## Key Insights From the Model

### 1. Location Matters

**Why water proximity dominates site selection:**
- Cooling accounts for 85% of water use
- Free/economizer cooling saves 50% vs. chillers
- Climate (Minnesota < Arizona) determines water needs

**Why energy proximity drives capacity:**
- Power transmission losses: 5-8%
- Voltage regulation costs
- Most cost-effective: on-site renewables or nearby generation

### 2. Growth Accelerates Concentration

**Early trend (2018-2024):** Growth spread across existing 13 sites

**Mid-term (2025-2040):** New hubs emerge at strategic water/power nodes

**Late-term (2050-2074):** Consolidated into 20-25 mega-facilities

**Implication:** Single facility failures become more critical to internet resilience

### 3. Efficiency Gains Buy Time

**Scenario comparison:**

| Growth Rate | 2050 Power | 2074 Power |
|------------|-----------|-----------|
| 18% (unbounded) | 266 GW | 945 GW |
| 10% (realistic) | 159 GW | 382 GW |
| 5% (breakthrough) | 92 GW | 128 GW |

**Gap:** 260 GW between baseline and breakthrough by 2050

**Conclusion:** Without efficiency revolution, hyperscale cloud is unsustainable

### 4. Water Wars Visible by 2040

**Southwest stress (Arizona, Nevada):**
- Colorado River already overallocated
- Datacenters compete with agriculture
- Predicted: 2035 facility expansion to Albuquerque requires new water rights

**Midwest advantage:**
- Great Lakes compact allows expansion
- Cold climate reduces cooling water
- Predicted: Minneapolis, Green Bay sites highly feasible

### 5. Renewable Energy Alignment

**Current (2024):** 69% CFE → achievable with grid mix

**2040:** 85% CFE → requires dedicated solar/wind farms

**2074:** 98% CFE → each major facility needs on-site generation

**Implication:** Datacenters become energy infrastructure, not just consumers
